Fog computing is a new computing model with limited resources located near the Internet of Things (IoT) devices 
to provide low transmission delay, mobility, and location awareness services. However, fog technology not only 
extends the cloud resources but also inherits its properties and security problems, such as device capture, man-
in-the-middle, secret key compromise, identity impersonation, and message replay attacks. These vulnerabilities 
pose signiﬁcant risks to the integrity of IoT-fog communications. Nevertheless, the existing mutual authentication 
approaches either suﬀer from high computational overheads or fail to provide secure mutual authentication. To 
address this gap, this paper introduces LAAKA, a Lightweight Anonymous Authentication and Key Agreement 
scheme. Considering the constrained resources of IoT and fog devices, LAAKA utilizes lightweight operations 
such as hash function and bitwise XOR. Its main objective is to facilitate mutual authentication and establish 
secure session keys between IoT devices and fog servers, making it useful for various IoT applications. We 
validate the robustness of LAAKA against various security threats by conducting comprehensive formal security 
analysis, including Burrows-Abadi-Needham (BAN) logic and Random Oracle Model (ROM), as well as informal 
analysis. The eﬃcacy of our scheme is further demonstrated through evaluation with the Scyther tool. Compared 
to other proposed authentication approaches, the results illustrate the superior performance and eﬃciency of 
our approach in enhancing the security features, minimizing the computational cost, and optimizing storage 
utilization.
1. Introduction
The rapidly increasing number of Internet of Things (IoT) devices 
results in generating massive amounts of data (Guo et al. (2020)). Due 
to the limited processing capacity of IoT devices, this generated data 
is usually submitted to the cloud servers for processing. Cloud com-
puting has high processing capacity and unbounded storage resources 
to execute the assigned tasks and store the received data eﬃciently 
(Almurisi and Tadisetty (2022); Ali and Sridevi (2023)). However, the 
cloud nodes are located physically far from the IoT data sources, leading 
to high network latency and thus aﬀecting the response time of the IoT 
application (Ali et al. (2021a)). Moreover, the centralized processing 
of tasks by the cloud makes it an unsuitable and ineﬃcient comput-
ing paradigm for processing location-aware and real-time applications. 
Thus, a new computing technology was introduced by Cisco in 2012 
(Jiang et al. (2020)) called fog computing to overcome the limitations 
of the cloud and shift its resources to the network edge near the IoT 
data sources. The features of fog computing help in reducing the com-

Despite fog computing being more secure than the cloud and pro-

still faces many security issues that should be mitigated (Guo et al. 
(2020)). Authentication of IoT and fog devices is considered one of the 
most critical security issues in fog architecture that should be consid-
ered. However, authentication of devices faces many challenges, such as 
location tracking (Nandy et al. (2019)), impersonating the device iden-
tity (Tu et al. (2018)), and capturing the device physically (Ometov et 
al. (2022)). These challenges result from insuﬃcient protection for the 
distributed IoT and fog devices and their constrained resources, which 
leads to penetration, destruction, and stealing of these devices easily 
(Roman et al. (2018)). Therefore, the following security requirements 
should be addressed in fog computing:
• Authentication: This essential security service prevents the attacker 
from pretending to be a legitimate IoT or fog device. However, the

limited computational capacity of the IoT and fog devices is a bar-
rier to implementing authentication in the IoT-fog environment. So, 
traditional methods such as public key cryptography-based meth-
ods are not preferred to be used (Mukherjee et al. (2017)).
• Anonymity: This security service is necessary to hide the identity of 
the devices during the authentication phase to prevent the device 
tractability by the attacker (Kaur et al. (2020)).
• Forward and backward secrecy: This service ensures that if an at-
tacker somehow obtains the current session key, it cannot be used 
to decrypt previous messages or future communications. (Weng et 
al. (2021); Kunal et al. (2019)).
• Secure communication: The transmitted sensitive IoT data must be 
conﬁdential and accessible by authorized devices only. This service 
is achieved by encrypting the exchanged data over the wireless 
communications between the IoT devices and fog nodes, which can 
be eavesdropped easily (AbuTaha et al. (2011)).
• Independency of session keys: This service ensures that compro-
mising a session key does not compromise other established session 
keys, as it generates distinct and random session keys for each de-
vice (Ibrahim (2016)).
As automation becomes increasingly integral across diverse do-
mains, including smart industrial settings (Panda et al. (2022)), smart 
home (Jan et al. (2022)), and healthcare systems (Chen et al. (2022)), 
the capability of embedded systems to autonomously gather and trans-
mit real-time data for intelligent decision-making has become crucial. 
This automation, which often involves direct communication between 
IoT devices and local fog or cloud servers, is carried out with mini-
mal human interference. In this context, the communication link from 
IoT to server is critical, enabling direct machine-to-machine interac-
tions and playing a main role in the automation environments. Ensuring 
the authenticity of communication partners is a key security concern in 
these IoT-fog communication frameworks, serving as a countermeasure 
against diﬀerent security threats like replay, man-in-the-middle, imper-
sonation, and denial-of-service (Wazid et al. (2020)).
The absence of robust authentication could allow malicious actors 
to disrupt the decision-making process, for instance, by feeding erro-
neous data to the controllers in an Industry 4.0 setting. However, the 
application of intensive cryptographic operations for authentication is 
impractical for IoT devices, which are constrained by their process-
ing power, communication bandwidth, storage capacity, and energy 
resources (Jan et al. (2022)). This requires the adoption of a mutual 
authentication and key agreement protocol that is not only lightweight 
but also eﬃcient, ensuring the preservation of security within IoT-fog 
communication networks.
Despite the numerous mutual authentication and key agreement 
protocols designed to secure IoT-fog communication, a majority of these 
protocols encounter several challenges. These include vulnerability to 
Ephemeral-Secret-Leakage (ESL) attack (Guo et al. (2020); Jia et al. 
(2019); Zhang et al. (2020)), Denial of Service (DoS) attack (Vinoth 
et al. (2020); Guo and Guo (2021); Wazid et al. (2019b)), susceptibil-
ity to device capture attack (Wang et al. (2020); Vinoth et al. (2020); 
Wazid et al. (2019b)), and a lack of user and device anonymity (Ibrahim 
(2016); Ma et al. (2019); Li et al. (2020); Das et al. (2019)). Further 
issues include the absence of Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) (Kalaria 
et al. (2021)), the risk of user and device traceability attack (Ibrahim 
(2016); Wazid et al. (2019b); Ma et al. (2019); Kalaria et al. (2021)), 
high communication latency (Hamada et al. (2022); Weng et al. (2021); 
Singh and Chaurasiya (2021)) and high computational cost (Wang et 
al. (2020); Amor et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2021b); Lansky et al. (2022)). 
Moreover, many of these protocols depend on the presence of a regis-
tration authority to establish and distribute the session keys between 
the IoT devices and fog servers (Rahman and Wen (2019); Weng et al. 
(2021); Wu et al. (2021a)), which can be a signiﬁcant limitation.
In contrast, some authentication protocols are designed based on 
lightweight operations, such as hash function and bitwise XOR, con-
sidering the limited resources of IoT and fog servers. However, these 
approaches also come with their own limitations. For instance, the 
protocol proposed by Abdussami et al. (2022) requires a cloud server 
for mutual authentication between IoT devices and fog servers, while 
Masud et al. (2021)’s scheme is vulnerable to key impersonation, mas-
querade gateway, and privileged insider attacks. Similarly, Suganthi et 
al. (2020)’s approach is vulnerable to impersonation and privileged in-
sider attacks, while Dhillon and Kalra (2017)’s approach is not resistant 
against user impersonation, and stolen mobile device attacks. It also 
does not provide user anonymity and session key agreement.
Although the literature proposes various authentication and key 
agreement protocols, a fully eﬀective solution for these vulnerabilities 
is still absent. Moreover, given the constrained computational, storage, 
and power capabilities of IoT and fog devices, designing an authen-
tication scheme that is both resource-eﬃcient and secure remains a 
challenge. This gap in the ﬁeld has motivated us to develop a ro-
bust, lightweight mutual authentication and key agreement protocol 
that overcomes these vulnerabilities and ensures secure communication 
between IoT devices and fog servers.
Therefore, in this paper, we aim to leverage the primary advantage 
of fog computing, which is minimizing the network delay for IoT data 
transmission. To this end, we strategically place the trusted party reg-
istration authority within the fog layer, close to the IoT devices and 
fog servers, rather than in the cloud. This setup leads us to introduce 
LAAKA, a Lightweight Anonymous Authentication and Key Agreement 
scheme. LAAKA is designed to address the vulnerabilities in existing 
authentication schemes by ensuring robust mutual authentication be-
tween IoT devices and fog servers. It facilitates creating secure session 
keys, generated through distinct random numbers, without the need 
to involve the registration authority. Furthermore, this scheme main-
tains low computational and storage requirements by employing only 
lightweight operations, such as hash functions and bitwise XOR.
We summarize the contributions of this work as follows:
• Propose an eﬃcient lightweight authentication scheme between 
the IoT devices and fog servers, using hash function and bitwise 
XOR operations.
• Implement the authentication mutually between the IoT devices 
and fog servers without the help of the Registration Authority.
• Establish a distinct secret session key for each connection of the 
IoT device with the fog server to secure their transmissions.
• Analyze the scheme’s performance using BAN logic, Random Ora-
cle Model (ROM), and Scyther tool, then compare it to the perfor-
mance of other existing authentication schemes in terms of security 
features, computational cost, and storage requirements.
The remainder of the paper is sketched as follows: Section 2 intro-
duces some related works. Section 3 presents the system architecture 
and threat models. Section 4 discusses the proposed authentication and 
key agreement scheme (LAAKA), while its security analysis (i.e., formal 
and informal) is conducted in Section 5. Section 6 shows the perfor-
mance analysis of our scheme and the results of comparison with other 
presented schemes. Finally, the work is concluded in Section 7 in addi-
tion to some suggestions for future directions.
2. Related work
This section shows some recently proposed mutual authentication 
and key agreement schemes in fog computing. Ali and Sridevi (2022)
designed an authentication mechanism that enables the IoT device to 
mutually authenticate with the fog server, using hash function and 
asymmetric key cryptography. However, this mechanism causes a high 
computational cost to achieve anonymity and provide secure transmis-
sion between the IoT and fog devices. Ibrahim (2016) presented a secure 
authentication protocol to enable the user and fog server to authen-
ticate each other with cooperation from the cloud as the registration

authority. This protocol also adopted hash function and asymmetric en-
cryption. Although the proposed work reduces the computational and 
storage cost, it does not provide identity anonymity, exposing it to the 
man-in-the-middle attack. Furthermore, the secret keys are stored in 
clear form in the user device’s memory and also in the fog server’s 
memory, causing the device capture attack to access this conﬁdential 
information. These limitations have been solved with the proposed au-
thentication approach by Rahman and Wen (2019), using hash-based 
message authentication code along with symmetric key encryption. De-
spite this approach being protected from the man-in-the-middle attack 
and providing the user identity anonymity, there are no details about 
how the secret key was shared previously between the fog user and 
the registration authority. Moreover, establishing the session key re-
quires the existence of the cloud as the registration authority, leading 
to more communication overhead and high network latency. One more 
disadvantage of this approach is that the long-term secret key is stored 
in clear form in the memory of the fog and user devices and used to 
generate the session key. In contrast, Hamada et al. (2022) enhanced 
this work by establishing the session key without resorting to the regis-
tration authority, using ECC operation and symmetric key encryption. 
However, this scheme is limited in the Canetti-Krawczyk (CK) threat 
model, where the secret keys can be extracted from the memory of the 
compromised or stolen user or fog device.
Wang et al. (2020) also used hash function and symmetric encryp-
tion in their proposed lightweight authentication scheme to generate 
dynamic session keys. However, many keys are used in this protocol 
while transmitting the authentication messages, causing high compu-
tational overhead and time consumption. Additionally, this protocol is 
not resistant to the device capture attack. The same cryptographic op-
erations have been used in the scheme proposed by Vinoth et al. (2020)
to implement multi-factor mutual authentication between the users and 
sensors in an industrial IoT environment. However, their scheme is in-
secure against device capture, denial of service, and replay attacks. 
Amor et al. (2017) designed a mutual authentication protocol in fog 
architecture, considering techniques such as using hash, ECC, and bilin-
ear pairing. This protocol is immune to replay, man-in-the-middle, and 
eavesdropping attacks. In contrast, it causes a high computational cost 
and suﬀerers from session key compromising and fog server Imperson-
ation attacks.
Lightweight authentication schemes often focus on the use of hash 
functions and bitwise XOR operations for enhanced eﬃciency. Weng et 
al. (2021) developed mutual authentication and key agreement between 
users and edge devices with fog servers, utilizing hash function and 
bitwise XOR operation to reduce computational costs and enhance secu-
rity. However, this scheme involves the cloud registration authority in 
session key generation, leading to increased load on the cloud, potential 
exposure of conﬁdential data, and added network delays. Additionally, 
the absence of timestamps in message exchanges makes it susceptible to 
replay attack. Similarly, Rana et al. (2021a) employed hash functions 
and bitwise XOR in developing a lightweight authentication protocol for 
IoT devices. As analyzed by Das et al. (2021), this protocol fails to eﬃ-
ciently protect the session key, does not oﬀer user anonymity, presents 
a risk of user traceability, and is vulnerable to ESL attack. Guo and 
Guo (2021) also adopted hash functions and XOR operations in their 
protocol for anonymous authentication and key a between mobile IoT 
devices and fog nodes. Despite its beneﬁts, this protocol faces several 
challenges, such as vulnerability to DoS attack, potential for user trace-
ability, risk of oﬄine password guessing, and insuﬃcient security for 
the shared session key.
Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) is widely used in fog computing 
authentication schemes along with hash functions and bitwise XOR op-
erations due to its suitability for devices with limited resources. Wazid 
et al. (2019b) presented a user authentication scheme called SAKA-FC, 
using secure hash function, bitwise XOR, and ECC operation. Although 
the SAKA-FC scheme provides robust security functionalities and low 
computational cost, it is still insecure against clogging, user imperson-
ation, traceability, and device capture attacks. Ali et al. (2021b) pre-
sented an authentication approach for smart devices in fog computing to 
overcome the vulnerabilities of Wazid et al. (2019b)’s scheme. Although 
this approach is more secure and robust against many attacks, it causes 
more computational cost and high delay due to involving the cloud 
server in the proposed approach. Patonico et al. (2019) also proposed 
an ECC-based protocol in fog computing, focusing on mutual authen-
tication and key agreement. This protocol ensures identity anonymity 
and secure session keys under the Canetti-Krawczyk (CK) threat model, 
but it doesn’t protect against replay and device capture/stolen attacks. 
Singh and Chaurasiya (2021) presented another lightweight authenti-
cation scheme using hash function and ECC operation. Despite its low 
computational cost, this scheme is vulnerable to internal spooﬁng at-
tacks in fog-cloud computing and requires the cloud as part of the 
authentication procedure, leading to a high load on the cloud and net-
work latency.
Various studies have focused on developing mutual authentication 
schemes independent of third-party involvement. Guo et al. (2020) pre-
sented an eﬃcient authentication scheme that excludes cloud servers 
as a third party. This scheme employs hash functions, symmetric poly-
nomials, and bitwise XOR operations, yet it remains vulnerable to ESL 
attack as per the Canetti-Krawczyk adversary model. Similarly, Gope 
(2019) introduced three privacy-preserving lightweight authentication 
protocols in fog-cloud environment. These protocols are applicable in 
three distinct scenarios: authentication of end devices by a centralized 
server, authentication among authenticated end devices themselves, 
and authentication of end devices by network access devices or base 
stations. Utilizing lightweight cryptographic techniques, including hash 
functions, bitwise XOR, and symmetric encryption (AES), these schemes 
oﬀer operational eﬃciency. However, they exhibit vulnerabilities to 
several security threats, such as capturing the end device or network 
access device, known session key, masquerading, and DoS attacks in 
addition to the absence of perfect forward secrecy. Kalaria et al. (2021)
also proposed mutual authentication and key exchange scheme between 
the fog users and servers, without involving the could server. Although 
this scheme uses only hash function and ECC operation, it does not oﬀer 
perfect forward secrecy feature, and is vulnerable to user traceability, 
data forgery, and oﬄine password guessing attacks.
Other studies have focused on implementing mutual authentication 
between IoT devices and fog servers in fog-driven healthcare systems. 
Jia et al. (2019) designed an authentication and key agreement proto-
col that uses bilinear pairings to enable mutual authentication between 
the IoT and fog devices in the healthcare system. This protocol uses the 
cloud as the third party and shares the generated session key with the 
IoT, fog, and cloud parties. Although this protocol is immune to man-
in-the-middle, message replay, and stolen device attacks, it increases 
the computational cost and transmission delay, which is unsuitable 
for limited resource devices and real-time applications. Both Chen et 
al. (2021) and Wu et al. (2021b) found that Jia’s scheme is vulnera-
ble to ESL attack, compromising session key security. They proposed 
enhanced schemes addressing this issue and ensuring secure communi-
cation but also increasing computational demands and communication 
latency due to reliance on cloud server while establishing the session 
key.
In the context of Internet of Vehicular (IoV) networks, Ma et al. 
(2019) proposed three-party authenticated key Agreement scheme be-
tween fog user, fog server, and could server in a fog-based vehicular 
IoT network. Their scheme increased the number of scalar point multi-
plications up to 17 and removed the bilinear pairings, compared with 
Jia’s scheme. However, this scheme doesn’t provide the user’s identity 
anonymity and untraceability features and is vulnerable to ESL attack 
(Zhang et al. (2020)). To overcome these limitations, Eftekhari et al. 
(2021) proposed an enhanced scheme that reduced the scalar point mul-
tiplications to nine. Despite this improvement, their scheme does not 
oﬀer perfect forward secrecy feature. Wazid et al. (2019a) introduced 
mutual authentication and key management protocol between vehicles

3. System architecture and threat model
This section presents the system architecture and deﬁnes two threat 
models to evaluate the security of our scheme.
3.1. System architecture
Fig. 1 presents the typical system architecture of fog computing that 
comprises the cloud, fog, and IoT devices layers. The topmost layer 
is the cloud, which has many data centers with unlimited computa-
tional and storage capacities. However, these data centers are located 
physically far from the IoT devices. In contrast, the fog layer is located 
between the cloud layer and IoT devices and includes one central node 
called the Registration Authority (𝑅𝐴) and many processing fog servers 
(𝐹) with limited resources. The registration authority is a trusted cen-
tral fog server that registers the IoT and fog devices securely in the fog 
layer. However, we assume that the registration authority is not faulty 
and is working in good condition close to the fog servers and connected 
with them by high-speed links, making the communication delay neg-
ligible in the fog layer. In contrast, the registration authority connects 
with the cloud over the Internet. The lowest layer of the architecture 
is the IoT devices layer, which involves various IoT devices (𝐷) such 
as vehicles, smartphones, robots, etc. The IoT devices are connected 
to the fog servers wirelessly, and both the IoT devices and fog servers 
are considered untrusted parties. Therefore, when the IoT device wants 
to interact securely with the fog server, they should authenticate each 
other without resorting to the registration authority.
3.2. Threat model
In this work, we adhere to the Dolev-Yao (DY) threat model (Dolev 
and Yao (1983)). Based on this model, we have the following assump-
tions:
• The network links between the IoT devices and fog servers are in-
secure and public.
Fig. 1. Fog computing architecture.
• The attacker has full control of the public links between the IoT 
devices and fog servers, which means that the attacker can replay, 
delay, eavesdrop, modify, delete, and reorder the transmitted mes-
sages over these links.
• The endpoints of the link (i.e., IoT device, fog server) are not 
trusted parties, while the registration authority is considered a 
trusted party and can not be penetrated.
We also consider the Canetti-Krawczyk (CK) adversary model 
(Canetti and Krawczyk (2001)). According to this model, there are the 
following assumptions:
• The adversary can get the secret keys and other information stored 
in the captured IoT device or fog server using a power analysis 
attack.
• The adversary can use the compromised data to apply many at-
tacks, such as session key exposure, man-in-the-middle, identity 
impersonation, and replay attacks.
4. Proposed scheme
This section presents LAAKA, a Lightweight Anonymous Authentica-
tion and Key Agreement scheme in fog-driven IoT systems. This scheme 
enables IoT devices and fog servers to authenticate each other with-
out the help of the registration authority. The notations adopted in this 
work and their meaning are illustrated in Table 2. The proposed scheme 
consists of the three following phases.
4.1. Initialization phase
The registration authority (RA) should do this phase before register-
ing the IoT and fog devices. RA generates a long-term master secret key 
(K) and store it securely in a protected place. It also chooses a secure 
hash function h(.). We assume that the registration authority is a fully 
trusted party in the fog layer, which stores all the information of the 
registered IoT devices and fog servers in a secure database.

tion authority when it joins the network as follows:

5. Security analysis
This section provides a comprehensive examination of the security 
measures within our scheme, demonstrating its robust defenses against 
various cyberattacks. Along with an informal security analysis that il-
lustrates the scheme’s resilience, we also explore more formalized meth-
ods. By using Burrows-Abadi-Needham (BAN) logic and Random Oracle 
Model (ROM), we conduct a rigorous security assessment to validate the 
theoretical soundness of our design. Additionally, we utilize the Scyther 
tool, a prominent tool in security analysis, to empirically evaluate the 
robustness of the proposed protocol, further solidifying its reliability 
and eﬀectiveness in real-world applications.

5.1. Informal security analysis
Our scheme (LAAKA) is robust against various cyberattacks, as ex-
plained below:
Man-in-The-Middle (MiTM) attack: This type of attack occurs 
when an unauthorized party intercepts the messages transmitted be-
tween the IoT device 𝐷𝑗and fog server 𝐹𝑖. The proposed protocol coun-
ters this threat by utilizing hash functions to create secret values (𝐶𝑑, 
𝐶𝑓, 𝐺𝑑, 𝐺𝑓, 𝑆𝐾). This construction ensures that reversing the hash 
to ﬁnd the original values is computationally intractable. Furthermore, 
the protocol employs comprehensive authentication checks, including 
the veriﬁcation of hash values, temporary identiﬁers, and timestamps. 
So, a MiTM attacker needs to forge these values accurately to imper-
sonate one of the parties, which should be diﬃcult without access to 
the secret values 𝐴𝑑, 𝐴𝑓, 𝐵𝑘. The session key (𝑆𝐾), derived from se-
curely exchanged random values (𝑟𝑑, 𝑟𝑓), further fortiﬁes the protocol. 
A MiTM attacker, despite intercepting messages, ﬁnds calculating the 
session key impossible due to the inaccessibility of these random val-
ues. Consequently, the integrity and authenticity of the communication 
remain intact, making the protocol highly resilient to MiTM attacks.
Replay attack: Occurs when an attacker intercepts a valid message 
and resends it later, intending to deceive the recipient into believing 
it’s legitimate. Our scheme counters this threat through multiple strate-
gies. Firstly, we use timestamps (𝑇𝑑, 𝑇𝑓, 𝑇𝑠) to ensure messages are 
time-sensitive; both the IoT device and the fog server check their fresh-
ness, dismissing any that fall outside a designated time window. Next, 
for each session, distinct random values 𝑟𝑑, 𝑟𝑓) are generated, adding 
another layer of complexity for any attacker trying to resend a previ-
ous message. The session key (𝑆𝐾) is calculated based on these random 
values and the timestamp 𝑇𝑠. So, it is a session-speciﬁc key and can’t 
be reused. Additionally, the protocol can detect any altered timestamps

using a bitwise XOR operation with hash values. These hash values in-

Stolen Veriﬁer attack: Occurs when an attacker obtains the veriﬁ-

the threat is mitigated by not storing the real identiﬁers (𝐼𝐷𝑑, 𝐼𝐷𝑓), 
which are essential for generating 𝐴𝑑or 𝐴𝑓. Consequently, even if an

enough details to impersonate a legitimate device. The protocol’s incor-

to the attacker adds to the complexity, as these numbers are required to

identiﬁer and the random numbers, will result in authentication failure. 
Without the correct random values, the attacker also can’t compute the 
proper session key, further hindering their impersonation attempts.
Key Exposure attack: In our proposal, the fog server generates a 
unique session key (𝑆𝐾) for each connection with an IoT device. This 
speciﬁcity ensures that the security of one session doesn’t compromise 
others. The session key is exclusively known to the fog server and the 
associated IoT device. Other parties, such as the Registration Author-
ity (𝑅𝐴), do not have access to this secret key. This restriction further 
isolates the session, ensuring that even if other parts of the system are 
compromised, the session key remains secure. The attacker’s inability to 
access the randomly generated numbers by the IoT device and the fog 
server during the authentication process prevents them from reverse-
engineering or otherwise exposing the session key.
Known Session Key attack: The adversary may obtain an old 
known session key to access the secret parameters and session keys 
of the subsequent communication sessions. According to our proposed 
approach, the session key is a hashed value of random numbers. It is 
computationally infeasible to know these numbers by obtaining the 
hash value because of the one-way characteristic of the hash function. 
However, the adversary is unable to get the current generated random 
numbers to deduce a new session key by knowing of previously compro-
mised keys. Consequently, our scheme is immune to the known session 
key attack.

5.3. Security proof using the random oracle model
To further prove the robustness of our proposed scheme, we intro-
duce a formal security analysis using the Random Oracle Model (ROM). 
Recognized as a crucial method for cryptographic veriﬁcation, the ROM 
model plays an essential role in demonstrating the resilience of crypto-
graphic protocols against sophisticated adversarial strategies (Koblitz 
and Menezes (2015)), especially in the ﬁelds of the Internet of Things 
(IoT) and fog computing.
To prove that our scheme is secure under ROM, we must prove 
that any adversary, particularly one that is bound by probabilistic 
polynomial-time (PPT) constraints, cannot compromise our scheme

that for each unique input, h(.) returns a random output from its out-
put space, and this output is consistent for repeated queries with the

collision-resistant and preimage-resistant properties that are crucial for 
our security analysis. Prior to proving the session key security of our

5.4. Security proof using Scyther tool
Scyther is an eﬃcient tool used to identify potential attacks and 
vulnerabilities in security protocols by automatically analyzing the pro-
tocols and scrutinizing their behavior (Cremers (2008)). It oﬀers more 
security features than other tools and veriﬁes the security properties 
of the protocol against a variety of attacks. Scyther operates based on 
a pattern reﬁnement algorithm, providing concise representations of 
(inﬁnite) sets of protocol cases. This functionality enables the tool to 
evaluate various types of attacks and potential protocol behaviors. The 
security protocols are veriﬁed against threats such as Dolev-Yao adver-
sary (Dolev and Yao (1983)) and internal attacks (Basin and Cremers 
(2014)). This tool has proven useful in checking several authentication 
protocols (Anand and Saifulla (2023); Kumar and Hussain (2023); Hos-
seinzadeh et al. (2020); Seno et al. (2021)). Leveraging the Security 
Protocol Description Language (SPDL), Scyther evaluates the validity of 
protocol security claims. Moreover, it automates their generation and 
validation processes, ensuring a thorough protocol assessment.
Fig. 5 shows the implementation code of our proposed protocol in 
Scyther, which includes two roles: the IoT device 𝐷𝑗and the fog server 
𝐹𝑖. The security claims of the roles within the protocol are described as 
follows (Rehman et al. (2021); Klevstad (2016)):
• Secrecy: This claim safeguards parameters that must be kept hid-
den from potential attackers. In our protocol, random numbers 
𝑟𝑑, 𝑟𝑓are deemed conﬁdential.
• Session-Key Reveal (SKR): This claim is used in the protocol to 
identify the session key 𝑆𝐾and claim that it is secret.
• Non-injective agreement (Niagree): The claim ensures that both 
𝐷𝑗and 𝐹𝑖agreed on the data values and veriﬁed the noninjective 
agreement, preventing any device injection attack.
• Non-injective synchronization (Nisynch): This claim considers 
the timestamps as nonces securely transmitted over public chan-
nels, thus preventing replay attacks.
• Alive: This claim ascertains the active participation of the coun-
terparties in the protocol (𝐷𝑗or 𝐹𝑖). If 𝐷𝑗completes a protocol 
run with 𝐹𝑖, then it conﬁrms that 𝐹𝑖has previously initiated the 
protocol.
• Weak agreement (Weakagree): This claim ensures that if 𝐷𝑗suc-
cessfully complete a protocol run with 𝐹𝑖, then 𝐹𝑖believes that 
it run recently the protocol with 𝐷𝑗. This claim conﬁrms that our 
protocol provides identiﬁer authentication and data source authen-
tication using 𝐴𝑑, 𝐴𝑓, which helps to prevent impersonation and 
Man-in-the-Middle attacks.

versely, a Fail status denotes the protocol’s failure to satisfy a speciﬁc

6.1. Security features
Table 3 shows the security features provided by our scheme LAAKA
compared with other introduced schemes. We use “Yes” to indicate that 
the security feature is provided by the corresponding scheme, whereas 
“No” indicates that the security feature is not provided by the scheme.
According to Table 3, all evaluated schemes, including ours, suc-
cessfully implement mutual authentication and oﬀer resistance to Man-
in-the-Middle (MiTM) attack. Weng et al. (2021)’s scheme is unique in 
its use of hash functions and bitwise XOR operations, yet it is the only 
one among them not resistant to replay attacks. Excluding Wazid et al. 
(2019a)’s scheme, all others eﬀectively defend against impersonation 
attacks targeting IoT devices and fog servers. However, the schemes in-
troduced by Weng et al. (2021) and Wazid et al. (2019a) do not provide 
protection against key exposure attacks. Additionally, Perfect Forward 
Secrecy (PFS) is a feature in all the reviewed schemes except for those 
by Rahman and Wen (2019) and Eftekhari et al. (2021).
The schemes proposed by Hamada et al. (2022) and Rahman and 
Wen (2019) assume that the secret shared key is stored in clear form 
in the device’s memory, making them vulnerable to the unknown key-
share attack and the device (i.e., IoT device, fog server) physical capture 
attack.
However, a common limitation of these schemes is the increased 
communication latency, attributed to the registration authority being 
located in the cloud layer, which is involved in device registration and 
authentication processes. In contrast, our proposed scheme (LAAKA) is 
more robust and reliable, oﬀering greater resistance against a wider 
range of well-known attacks and enhanced security features.

7. Conclusion and future work
This study introduced LAAKA, a Lightweight Anonymous Authen-
tication and Key Agreement scheme designed for the IoT-fog environ-
ment. LAAKA ensures an optimal balance between lightweight opera-
tion and robust security, utilizing hash functions and bitwise XOR for 
mutual authentication between IoT devices and fog servers. Moreover, 
it eﬀectively establishes secure session keys without relying on a trusted 
registration authority, situated within the fog layer to minimize latency. 
We have accurately designed our scheme to provide secure commu-
nication within the context of the DY threat model and improve the 
conﬁdentiality of the session key under the CK threat model. Another 
key feature of LAAKA is its emphasis on anonymity, ensuring user pri-
vacy by concealing device identities. These strengths have been proved 
through extensive formal (i.e., BAN, and ROM) and informal security 
analyses, demonstrating the protocol’s eﬀectiveness in preventing a 
wide array of potential security threats. Another software-based analy-
sis has been done using the Scyther tool to conﬁrm the robustness of our 
scheme against a wide range of attacks and vulnerabilities. Moreover, 
comparative evaluations with existing authentication schemes have il-
lustrated our protocol’s distinct advantages in terms of security feature 
set, computational overhead, and storage requisites. However, the re-
search ﬁeld is ever-evolving, and our eﬀorts to reﬁne and optimize IoT 
security remain ongoing.
In future work, we plan to enhance the robustness of our system 
by decentralizing the registration process. This strategy aims to reduce 
vulnerabilities linked to a single point of failure, thereby improving the 
overall network reliability. Moreover, addressing the mobility of IoT de-
vices is a key focus; we plan to reﬁne our scheme to eﬀectively manage 
device movement. This enhancement will ensure consistent authentica-
tion integrity while preserving operational eﬃciency. Another future 
plan is extending the scheme to implement IoT users, incorporating 
advanced techniques like multi-factor and biometric veriﬁcation meth-
ods. This expansion will also include inter-fog device-to-device (D2D) 
authentication, ensuring secure communication among all involved en-
tities. Additionally, this scheme can be implemented and evaluated in a 
real testbed environment with actual resource-constrained IoT devices, 
local fog devices, and cloud server instances.
